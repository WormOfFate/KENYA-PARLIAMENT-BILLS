# **The Judiciary Reform Act 1, 2026**

**A Bill for an Act of Parliament to give effect to aspects of judicial authority, structure, qualifications, and appointments consistent with Chapter Ten of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010, and to provide for related matters.**

**ENACTED by the Parliament of Kenya as follows:**

### Part I – Preliminary
**1. Short title and commencement**  
This Act may be cited as the Judiciary Implementation Act, 2026, and shall come into operation upon publication in the Gazette.

**2. Interpretation**  
In this Act, unless the context otherwise requires—  
“Chief Justice” has the meaning assigned in Article 163 of the Constitution;  
“JSC” means the Judicial Service Commission established under Article 171 of the Constitution;  
“FURAHA principles” refers to the guiding objectives and limitations outlined in the proposed Article IV (Sections 1–2) adapted to Kenya’s constitutional framework of judicial authority derived from the people (Article 159), independence (Article 160), and the rule of law.  
All provisions shall be read subject to the supremacy of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010 (Article 2).

**3. Purpose**  
The purpose of this Act is to:  
(a) Strengthen the application of judicial authority to determine truth in controversies, apply law impartially, protect the innocent, punish the guilty, and safeguard the Constitution, individual liberty, and the rule of law, consistent with Article 159;  
(b) Reinforce core limitations on judicial power, including no legislative or executive authority, narrow interpretation in favour of liberty where ambiguous, and respect for constitutional text and statutory intent;  
(c) Provide a framework for court structure, qualifications, and appointments that aligns with the Constitution while incorporating enhanced standards for integrity, experience, and public accountability.

### Part II – General Principles and Limitations
**4. Judicial Principles**  
(1) Judicial authority shall be exercised in accordance with Article 159 of the Constitution and the principles in Section 1 of the referenced Article IV, adapted as follows:  
   - Courts shall determine truth insofar as necessary to resolve matters before them.  
   - Judges shall apply the law impartially to the facts.  
   - Decisions shall protect constitutional rights and the rule of law.  
   - Proceedings shall guard against malicious conduct while ensuring due process.  

(2) Limitations:  
   - Courts shall not exercise legislative or executive powers (consistent with separation of powers under Articles 1, 94, 129, and 160).  
   - Judicial decisions bind parties to the case and have persuasive effect; binding precedent applies only as per established hierarchy and superior court rulings.  
   - In ambiguity, courts shall interpret narrowly in favour of individual liberty and rights under the Bill of Rights.  
   - Courts shall not substitute judgment for clear constitutional text or lawful executive discretion except where clearly unconstitutional.  
   - Rulings are limited to the jurisdiction and relief necessary for the parties, except as authorised by statute.

**5. Standing and Justiciability**  
Legal standing requires personal injury or sovereign interest. Constitutional challenges to new laws or policies shall generally be raised within three months in a court of competent jurisdiction, subject to Article 165 (High Court) and appellate processes. This does not limit ongoing rights under Articles 22 and 23.

### Part III – Court Structure (Initial Provisions)
**6. Superior Courts**  
This Act affirms the structure under Articles 162–165: Supreme Court, Court of Appeal, and High Court. Panels and benches shall operate as provided by law and rules of court, with the Chief Justice exercising administrative leadership.

**7. Subordinate Courts**  
Subordinate courts, including Magistrates’ Courts, shall serve as primary courts of first instance, with jurisdiction over municipal infractions, misdemeanours, petty crimes, small claims, and matters assigned by law, consistent with Article 169.

**8. Chief Magistrate’s Courts**  
Each county shall strengthen the role of the Chief Magistrate’s Court as a key court of first instance. Panels may include magistrates sitting with assessors or in accordance with jury-like mechanisms where statutorily provided, subject to further legislation.

### Part IV – Qualifications and Accountability (Initial Framework)
**9. General Qualifications for Judges and Magistrates**  
A person qualifies for appointment as a Judge or Magistrate if they:  
(a) Are a Kenyan citizen (aligning with constitutional requirements; native-born emphasis to be considered in vetting);  
(b) Meet the experience thresholds under Article 166 and JSC guidelines (e.g., at least seven years’ practice as an advocate for relevant courts);  
(c) Have no prior criminal conviction and demonstrate integrity (enhanced vetting under this Act);  
(d) Commit to making annual financial declarations public while in office, in addition to existing asset declarations under the Constitution and Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission laws.  

Appellate judges shall generally have served in inferior courts.

**10. Supreme Court and Higher Qualifications**  
Supreme Court Justices shall additionally demonstrate:  
- Substantial judicial experience (at least seven years as a Judge, with service in different courts);  
- Constitutional knowledge and personal integrity;  
- Age and maturity considerations consistent with high office.  

During JSC processes and parliamentary approval (where applicable), candidates may be required to address prior rulings.

**11. Appointment Process**  
Appointments shall follow Articles 166 and 172: President appoints Chief Justice, Deputy Chief Justice, and Supreme Court Judges on JSC recommendation, with National Assembly approval where required. This Act emphasises non-justiciable executive/legislative roles in appointments while upholding transparency and merit.

**12. Terms and Tenure**  
Tenure shall be during good behaviour, with retirement ages and removal processes as per Articles 167–168. This Act does not alter constitutional tenure but supports secure compensation that shall not be diminished during service.

**13. Initial Accountability Measures**  
(1) The JSC shall develop procedures for addressing conflicts of interest, malpractice, or procedural errors, including appeals to higher benches.  
(2) The Chief Justice may exercise suspension powers for cause, subject to due process and referral to JSC or relevant bodies.  
(3) Impeachment/removal follows Article 168.  
(4) Disciplinary records shall be published promptly where appropriate, subject to privacy and ongoing proceedings.  

**14. Deputy Judges/Magistrates**  
Provision is made for the appointment of qualified deputies or acting judicial officers for limited terms to assist with caseloads, subject to Chief Justice/JSC approval and public notification for challenges.

### Part V – Miscellaneous
**15. Transition and Savings**  
Existing judicial officers and structures continue subject to this Act and the Constitution. The JSC shall align rules and guidelines within six months.

**16. Regulations**  
The Chief Justice and JSC may make regulations for the better carrying out of this Act, consistent with the Constitution.

**17. Consequential Amendments**  
The Judges and Magistrates Vetting Act, Judicial Service Act, and other relevant laws shall be amended to align with this Act where necessary.
