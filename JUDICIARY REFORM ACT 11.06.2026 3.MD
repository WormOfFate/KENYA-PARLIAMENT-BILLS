# **The Judiciary Reform Act, 2026 (No. 3)**

**A Bill for an Act of Parliament to give effect to court structures, jurisdiction, judicial accountability, the Attorney Bar, and to amend the Penal Code (Cap 63) and related statutes on classification of crimes, capital punishment, theft, restitution, and sentencing, consistent with Articles 26, 50, 159, 160, 165, 169, 170 and Chapter Ten of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010.**

**ENACTED by the Parliament of Kenya as follows:**

### Part I – Preliminary
**1. Short title and commencement**  
This Act may be cited as the Judiciary Reform Act, 2026 (No. 3), and shall come into operation upon publication in the Gazette, except for provisions relating to capital punishment which shall take effect upon the necessary constitutional and legislative adjustments or as determined by the Supreme Court.

**2. Interpretation**  
This Act shall be read together with the Judiciary Implementation Act, 2026 (No. 1) and the Judiciary Reform Act, 2026 (No. 2). All provisions are subject to the supremacy of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010.

**3. Purpose**  
The purpose of this Act is to—  
(a) Complete the operational framework for court structures, jurisdiction, and accountability;  
(b) Strengthen the Attorney Bar and public service obligations;  
(c) Amend the Penal Code and related laws to introduce clear classification of crimes, capital offences, theft and restitution principles consistent with the principles in the referenced Article IV while respecting constitutional safeguards under Article 26 (Right to life) and Article 50 (Fair trial).

### Part II – Court Structures and Jurisdiction (Consolidation)
**4. Federal/State Analogue Structures**  
This Act affirms and operationalises the court structure under Articles 162–165 and 169:  
- Supreme Court (13 Justices where feasible, panels as per rules);  
- Court of Appeal and High Court;  
- Magistrates’ Courts, with strengthened County Chief Magistrates’ Courts consisting of panels of three Magistrates sitting with jurors where applicable.  

**5. Jurisdiction**  
(1) Jurisdiction follows Articles 165, 169 and this Act, aligned with the principles in the referenced Article IV Section 5.  
(2) Legal standing requires personal or sovereign injury. Constitutional challenges to new laws/policies shall generally be filed within three months in the appropriate court.  
(3) Inferior courts refer matters beyond their jurisdiction upwards.

### Part III – Judicial Accountability and Administration
**6. Enhanced Accountability**  
(1) The Judicial Service Commission (JSC) and Chief Justice shall operationalise suspension, disciplinary, and impeachment processes consistent with Articles 167–168 and the principles in referenced Article IV Section 3.4.  
(2) Disciplinary records shall be published within timelines specified in the referenced principles, subject to privacy and fairness.  
(3) Deputy Judges and acting judicial officers may be appointed for limited terms with public notice for challenges.

**7. Chief Justice and Administrative Roles**  
The Chief Justice shall exercise administrative leadership, including appointment of Treasurer and Secretary roles for financial and record integrity, consistent with referenced Section 4.7.

### Part IV – The Attorney Bar
**8. Regulation of Advocates**  
(1) The Federal analogue (national level) and County structures shall regulate the Bar through the Law Society of Kenya and relevant statutes.  
(2) Admission requires demonstrated knowledge of the Constitution, applicable laws, and ethical standards.  
(3) Every certified Advocate shall register for court-appointed representation for indigent persons, with allocation prioritising proximity and workload limits. Reasonable compensation shall be provided.  
(4) Courts retain disciplinary powers over Advocates appearing before them, including suspension or disbarment.

### Part V – Amendment of the Penal Code – Classification of Crimes and Capital Punishment
**9. Amendment of the Penal Code**  
The Penal Code (Cap. 63) is amended by inserting the following new provisions after Section 40 (or as appropriately placed by the Kenya Law Reform Commission) and by consequential adjustments to sentencing and procedure sections.

**New Section 6: Classification of Crimes and Capital Punishment**

**6.1 Classes of Crime**  
There shall be four primary classes of crime:  
1. Capital Crimes: offences that, upon conviction, shall attract the death sentence due to their grave threat to the continuity of community and society.  
2. Theft: crimes involving wrongful acquisition of property, capital or currency through force, deception, coercion or trickery (except lawful recovery from a criminal in the act).  
3. Felonies: serious crimes against national or county law requiring incarceration, and which may attract fines and/or corporal punishment.  
4. Misdemeanours: lesser offences against public order, civil compliance and social intercourse, which may attract fines and/or corporal punishment.  

The primary distinction between felonies and misdemeanours lies in the specific statutory provisions and penalties determined by the Court.

**6.2 Capital Crimes**  
The following offences, when proven beyond reasonable doubt in accordance with Article 50, shall be punishable by capital punishment:  
1. Murder, including abortion, where the core fact is the unjust taking of human life (subject to Article 26 constitutional safeguards and medical exceptions as provided by law).  
2. Robbery with violence, including rape, where the core fact is the use of violence to obtain material or sexual benefit or to further another crime.  
3. Human trafficking, kidnapping, hostage-taking, or trade in human beings, organs or body parts.  
4. Conspiracy, incitement, aiding or abetting the commission of a capital crime.  
5. False teaching of religious or moral authority that incites suicide or capital crimes, or proven witchcraft involving harm.  
6. False witness or corrupt judgment that results in the death of an innocent person.  
7. Treason, insurgency, or terrorism involving violence against the people or political rights.  
8. Commercial trade or publication of pornography, prostitution, bestiality, paedophilia; or intentional transmission of serious sexually transmitted diseases.  
9. Dereliction of duty or gross negligence by public officers that enables a capital crime.  
10. Drug trafficking or mutilation/habituation of minors.  

States/Counties and Parliament may by law define subclasses permitting lesser penalties in appropriate mitigating circumstances.

**6.3 Sentencing Discretion and Defences**  
(1) Courts and Juries retain discretion to impose the death penalty only where facts strictly match the capital criteria.  
(2) The State retains the right to use lawful lethal force in defence of the community.  
(3) Participants in mob violence bear collective responsibility but may plead just defence where the victim was in the act of committing a capital crime.  
(4) Citizens using lethal force in defence of others against a capital crime shall be recognised as national heroes and afforded full legal protection.

**6.4 Method of Execution**  
(1) Capital punishment shall be executed as follows (subject to humane standards and regulations by the Cabinet Secretary responsible for justice):  
   (a) Public firing squad of ten (10) officers, with up to two immediate family members or clan elders of the victim invited to participate; or  
   (b) Public bare-back lashing (maximum 39 lashes with regulated sisal rope).  
   Followed by hanging by the neck until death, in underwear (women in a single-piece dress), at a designated prison ground or public square as specified by law. Executions occur at dawn, with body displayed until dusk as a deterrent.  
(2) Persons below 18 years at the time of offence are exempt from public hanging.  
(3) Multiple convicts may be executed together, not exceeding ten at a time.

**6.5 Theft and Restitution**  
(1) Theft includes illegal seizure, fraud, corruption, usury (with clear evidence of malice), and robbery.  
(2) Upon conviction, the offender shall make restitution of four (4) to seven (7) times the value unjustly obtained.  
   - The victim receives 1.2 to 4 times the loss.  
   - The remainder is distributed equally among the Judiciary (waivable), prosecution (waivable), and material witnesses/reporters.  
   Waived portions revert to the victim.  
(3) In cases of corrupt judgment or false witness, liability is doubled.  
(4) Restitution may be enforced through asset seizure, liens, or labour during imprisonment. Excess recovered after full payment reverts to the offender.  
(5) Parliament or County Assemblies may set minimum thresholds for minor thefts to limit restitution to 1.2 times the loss.

**10. Consequential Amendments to Penal Code and Other Laws**  
The Penal Code, Criminal Procedure Code, and Prisons Act are amended to align sentencing, appeals, and execution procedures with this Act, subject to Article 26 of the Constitution and any Supreme Court declarations on the death penalty.

### Part VI – Miscellaneous
**11. Transition and Implementation**  
The JSC, Chief Justice, and Attorney-General shall, within eighteen (18) months, develop rules, training for Juries of Elders, and guidelines for implementation. Existing sentences and procedures continue until aligned.

**12. Regulations**  
The Chief Justice and Cabinet Secretary may make regulations for the effective implementation of this Act.

**13. Savings and Supremacy**  
Nothing in this Act shall be construed as overriding the Constitution. In case of any inconsistency, the Constitution prevails. This Act encourages public participation and stakeholder consultation prior to full operationalisation of capital punishment provisions.
