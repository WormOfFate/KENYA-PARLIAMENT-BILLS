# **The Judiciary Reform Act 2, 2026**

**A Bill for an Act of Parliament to give effect to enhanced due process, jury procedures, trial rights, and sentencing mechanisms in the Judiciary, consistent with Articles 50, 159, 160, 165, 169 and Chapter Ten of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010, and to provide for connected purposes.**

**ENACTED by the Parliament of Kenya as follows:**

### Part I – Preliminary
**1. Short title and commencement**  
This Act may be cited as the Judiciary Reform Act, 2026 (No. 2), and shall come into operation upon publication in the Gazette or on such later date as the Chief Justice may appoint by notice.

**2. Interpretation**  
In this Act—  
“Jury of Elders” means a panel of citizens selected in accordance with Section 7 of this Act;  
“Due Process” bears the meaning in Article 50 of the Constitution and is further elaborated in this Act;  
“Principal judicial officer” includes a Judge, Magistrate or Chief Magistrate;  
This Act shall be read together with the Judiciary Implementation Act, 2026 (No. 1) and subject to the supremacy of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010.

**3. Purpose**  
The purpose of this Act is to—  
(a) Strengthen due process and fair hearing rights under Article 50;  
(b) Establish a Jury of Elders system to enhance public participation in the administration of justice consistent with Article 159(1)(c) and (d);  
(c) Provide clear procedures for trials, sentencing, speedy justice, and emergency relief while preserving judicial independence and impartiality.

### Part II – Due Process and Fair Trial Rights
**4. Elaboration of Due Process**  
(1) All courts shall ensure trials are fully adversarial and uphold the rights in Article 50, including:  
   (a) The accused shall be promptly informed of the charge in a language they understand;  
   (b) The right to face accusers, cross-examine witnesses and evidence;  
   (c) The right to procure witnesses and evidence in defence;  
   (d) Access to all exculpatory evidence in the possession of the prosecution or State;  
   (e) The right to challenge the validity of the law itself where relevant;  
   (f) The prosecution bears the burden of proof beyond reasonable doubt in criminal matters (or on a balance of probabilities in civil matters).  

(2) No person shall be convicted or held liable except upon personal admission or after due process resulting in a finding of guilt.  

(3) All proceedings shall be public unless the court, for compelling reasons (protection of witnesses, national security, or privacy of minors), orders otherwise, with reasons recorded.

**5. Speedy Justice**  
(1) Arraignment shall occur within forty-eight (48) hours of arrest (excluding weekends and public holidays).  
(2) Trial shall commence within sixty (60) days of arrest, subject to reasonable extensions granted by a superior judicial officer for good cause.  
(3) Judgment shall be delivered within thirty (30) days of the close of hearing, extendable by a further fourteen (14) days with recorded reasons.  
(4) First appeals shall be filed within fourteen (14) days of judgment. Subsequent appeals require new material evidence or a more favourable change in law.  
(5) The Chief Justice shall issue Practice Directions to enforce these timelines and monitor compliance.

**6. Multiple Charges and Continuity**  
(1) Multiple charges arising from the same facts or transaction may be tried together.  
(2) Once a trial has commenced in a court, no new charges for the same conduct shall be introduced in that court except through appeal processes.  
(3) An accused may begin serving sentence on resolved charges while remaining charges proceed.

### Part III – Jury of Elders
**7. Establishment and Eligibility**  
(1) There is established a Jury of Elders system to promote public participation in justice.  
(2) Every Kenyan citizen who—  
   (a) is at least forty (40) years of age; and  
   (b) has no criminal conviction within the preceding seven (7) years,  
   shall be eligible for jury service.  

(3) Jury service shall last no more than two (2) months per year and shall be reasonably compensated from funds appropriated by Parliament.

**8. Selection and Challenges**  
(1) Jurors shall be selected by random draw from the register maintained by the relevant County or Court registry.  
(2) Parties shall have—  
   (a) up to two peremptory challenges; and  
   (b) unlimited challenges for cause.  
(3) The Trial of all crimes (except impeachment) and significant civil matters shall be by Jury of Elders unless the accused (in criminal cases) or both parties (in civil cases) knowingly waive the right in open court.

**9. Composition of Juries**  
- Criminal trials: 6–12 jurors.  
- Civil trials: 4–8 jurors.  
- Grand Juries (for serious felonies and capital offences): 18–24 jurors.  
The principal judicial officer shall vote only as tie-breaker where required.

**10. Role of Jury**  
(1) The Jury shall determine guilt or liability by majority vote based on evidence and personal conviction.  
(2) Jurors may, through the Court Clerk, seek clarifications on facts, law, or procedure, or request additional evidence within the scope of existing claims. Such questions shall form part of the public record (anonymised where necessary).  
(3) In criminal trials, the prosecution must disclose exculpatory evidence to the Jury.

### Part IV – Sentencing Procedures
**11. Sentencing Process**  
(1) Upon a guilty verdict, the victim (where applicable) and the accused shall submit victim impact and mitigation statements to be read in open court.  
(2) The principal judicial officer shall prepare a proposed sentence per charge and submit it to the Jury together with reasons.  
(3) The Jury shall deliberate in private and approve the sentence by a vote of at least two-thirds minus one.  
   - The Jury may reject the proposed sentence by two-thirds vote and request lighter or harsher terms, giving brief reasons.  
   - After three rejections, the statutory minimum (if any) applies, or no sentence if none exists.  
   - Sentences below statutory minimum require unanimous Jury approval.  

(4) The final sentence and Jury reasons shall be recorded and form part of appeal records.

**12. Grand Jury Indictment**  
Serious felonies and capital offences shall require indictment by a Grand Jury, which shall consider evidence presented by the prosecution and any exculpatory material.

### Part V – Emergency Docket and Interim Relief
**13. Emergency Constitutional Docket**  
(1) The Supreme Court, Court of Appeal, and High Court shall maintain an Emergency Docket for urgent applications involving substantial constitutional questions, threats to public order, or judicial integrity.  
(2) Applications shall be assessed on the likelihood of success, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and public interest.  
(3) Allocation shall be by random lot among available judicial officers.  
(4) A 24-hour automatic freeze may apply upon docketing in appropriate cases.  
(5) Emergency rulings are temporary and shall proceed to the regular docket for full hearing, with provision for amici curiae.  
(6) The Chief Justice may issue Binding Question Orders (up to three focused questions) to narrow issues for urgent determination.

### Part VI – Miscellaneous
**14. Records and Transparency**  
All regular judicial proceedings, evidence, and non-deliberative minutes shall be public records. Disciplinary proceedings against judicial officers shall be published promptly where adverse findings are made, subject to Article 168 processes.

**15. Attorney Bar and Court-Appointed Advocates**  
The Act reinforces the regulation of Advocates under existing law and mandates a public service obligation for court-appointed representation for indigent persons consistent with Article 50(2)(h) and (g).

**16. Transition**  
The Judicial Service Commission and Chief Justice shall, within twelve (12) months, develop rules, guidelines, and a national Jury register to operationalise this Act. Pilot programmes may commence in selected counties.

**17. Regulations**  
The Chief Justice, in consultation with the Judicial Service Commission and the Attorney-General, may make rules for the better carrying into effect of this Act.

**18. Consequential Amendments**  
The Criminal Procedure Code, Civil Procedure Act, Magistrates’ Courts Act, and other relevant statutes shall be amended to align with this Act.
